## 📝 Post Caption (Main Text)

A grain that survived Ice Ages is now outselling quinoa in European health stores.

Let that sit for a second.

Indian millet — the same crop that tribal farmers in Madhya Pradesh have grown for 3,000 years without a single drop of synthetic fertilizer — is quietly becoming the world's most sought-after superfood.

And most people haven't even heard of it yet.

Here are 5 things about Indian millet that genuinely stopped me in my tracks:

---

**1. It grows where nothing else will.**

Kodra (Kodo millet) and Kutki (Little millet) — known as Varagu in Tamil and Samai in South India — thrive in poor, rocky, rain-fed soil with as little as 400mm of annual rainfall. No irrigation. No synthetic inputs.

In a world facing a freshwater crisis, this is not a food story. It's a climate story.

---

**2. Ragi's glycemic index is around 54 — half that of white bread.**

Ragi (Finger millet) — called Kelvaragu in Tamil, Ragulu in Telugu, Mandua in Uttarakhand — has one of the lowest glycemic responses of any staple grain. White bread sits at ~75. White rice at ~73.

For the 537 million people globally living with diabetes, this isn't a food trend. It's a life-changing alternative.

---

**3. Bajra has 2–3x more iron per 100g than spinach.**

Pearl millet — Bajra in Hindi and Marathi, Kambu in Tamil, Sajje in Kannada — contains approximately 8–9mg of iron per 100g. Fresh spinach offers 2–4.5mg.

Nearly 2 billion people worldwide suffer from anaemia. The answer may have been sitting in Indian fields for centuries.

---

**4. The UN declared 2023 the International Year of Millets — and India led it.**

Over 70 countries participated. India's millet exports crossed USD 75 million in 2022–23, a nearly 20% jump over the prior year. And this is just the beginning of what's possible.

---

**5. And here is the statistic that genuinely shook me:**

India produces over 170 lakh tonnes of millets annually — contributing roughly 20% of global millet production.

Yet globally, most people have only ever encountered one millet: Jowar (Sorghum). The rest — Bajra, Ragi, Kangni (Foxtail), Sanwa (Barnyard), Kodra, Kutki, Cheena (Proso) — remain largely invisible outside the subcontinent.

That is one of the most underutilized nutritional libraries on Earth.

---

We are standing at the beginning of a genuine global food shift.

Not driven by marketing. Driven by climate pressure, health data, and the quiet wisdom of smallholder farmers who never needed a food scientist to validate what they already knew.
